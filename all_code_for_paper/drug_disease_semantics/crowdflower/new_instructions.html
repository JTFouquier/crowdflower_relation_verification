Title: Verify a drug-disease relationship in a sentence

Overview

Biomedical literature contains a wealth of information that remains locked in a text based format. In order to be able to effectively search all this knowledge, the information needs to be represented in a structured way. In this task, you will be helping scientists organize biomedical knowledge by determining the structured relationships between drugs and diseases.

The Task

  1. Read a sentence. One drug and one disease will be highlighted in the sentence. "Disease" may refer to a broad range of maladies including things like "obesity" and "headaches".
  2. Determine whether the sentence claims the drug and the disease are related.
  3. If the drug and disease are related, then select a choice that describes how they are related.

What each choice means

  • Yes, the sentence specifically describes how these concepts are related. The sentence clearly states that there is a relationship between the drug and the disease.
    • Example: "Rasperger's syndrome is a known complication of fortorzabine therapy."
  • Yes, the sentence presents a specific idea about how the concepts might be related. The sentence thinks there might be a link between the drug and the disease, but whether the link actually exists is unknown.
    • Example: "Further studies are necessary to elucidate whether use of ertanzol increases the risk of Porzine disease."
  • A specific claim is not made, but I think that a relationship is implied by the sentence. The sentence does not explicitly state that the drug and disease are related, but we can infer that the relationship exists because it is implied.
    • Example: "Probanmab prevents relapse of renoparcinoma after patients are taken off of querbitine." Here the drug "probanmab" is what is actually preventing "renoparcinoma" from reoccuring, but we can infer that "querbitine" is also playing some role because it only works if patients stop taking "querbitine".
  • The sentence specifically states that these concepts are not related, or that no evidence was found to support the relationship. The sentence says that there is no relationship between the drug and the disease, or that there is evidence against the relationship, or a lack of evidence supporting the link.
    • Example: "Our data demonstrate that there was no correlation between plobatinab use and incidence of Torwer's syndrome."
  • No, although these two concepts show up in the same sentence, nothing about the sentence suggests that they are related. The drug and disease are not related at all; they just simply show up in the same sentence.
    • Example: "In this study we looked at the effect of 4-3-bomanol on asthma in patients who also had cancer, kidney disease, and arthritis." No relationship exists between "4-3-bomanol" and "kidney disease"; the unlucky patients just happened to have multiple diseases.
  • [Drug] worsens or can sometimes cause [disease]. The drug can cause the disease to worsen in severity or frequency, or that the drug sometimes causes the disease (e.g., as a side effect) in people, animals, or cells.
    • Example: "Patients recieving oxcarbazepine often develop ertineoma as a side effect."
  • [Drug] improves or can be used as part of a treatment for [disease]. The drug causes an improvement of the disease or can be used as part of a treatment for the disease in people, animals, or cells.
    • Example: "After giving patients quertivib, we saw an immediate decrease in the severity of their muscle spasms."
